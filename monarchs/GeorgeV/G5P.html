George V Pennies

One of the most involved and numismatically interesting series of all the 20th Century predecimal coins. Straightforward single issue types are in the minority. I have deliberately omitted the more exotic coins that we mere mortals are unlikely to ever come across. There is plenty of information out there about the 1933 penny, for instance, and I see no benefit in repeating it here. This is intended to be a guide for the collector, after all.

Specifications

  • Diameter: 31mm
  • Weight: 9.4g
  • Alloys (up to 1922): Copper 95%, Tin 4%, Zinc 1%
  • Alloys (from 1926): Copper 95.5%, Tin 3%, Zinc 1.5%
  • Possibly some other variations

1911

All 1911 denominations have two obverse types:

  • ■ Flat Neck
  • ■ Hollow Neck

The rare Hollow Neck variety has a distinct diagonal indentation toward the back of the neck, roughly following the position of the tendon, whereas the King’s neck on the commoner type is sculpted smoothly. The scarce Hollow Neck is often referred to as Gouby X and is classified as Obverse 1* in the new Freeman edition, with under 50 known specimens.

1912

Minted at Tower Hill in London (no mintmark), and by The Mint, Birmingham (successors to Ralph Heaton and Sons), indicated by the small H mintmark in the left of the exergue. The Birmingham Mint also produced a large number of bronze blanks for farthings, halfpennies, and pennies from 1912 to 1919.

  • ■ 1912 – London Mint
  • ■ 1912 H – Birmingham Mint
1912 Penny Varieties
Obverse: London Mint Reverse
Obverse: Birmingham Mint (H) Reverse

Difficult to find at this level – the vast majority of H and KN pennies are really much of a muchness. The 1912 H in particular is much more common than it was while circulating at its natural level; as a well-known variety, a higher proportion of these were rescued from the decimalisation melting pot than London Mint 1912 pennies, so they are commoner now than they have ever been.

1913

Another classic crossover mule. 1913 began with 1+A and finished with 2+B. In between, probably by mistake, a few 1+B and 2+A coins slipped through.

1918

Three Mints took part in the production of pennies in 1918:

  • ■ Tower Hill Mint, London (no mintmark)
  • ■ Ralph Heaton & Sons, Birmingham (H mintmark)
  • ■ The Kings Norton Metal Company, Birmingham (KN mintmark)

In both 1918 and 1919, the H and KN pennies suffered from the same “rescue rate” phenomenon as the 1912 H penny. As a result, they are very common in low circulated grades, but in top grade they are extraordinarily difficult and highly collectable — textbook examples of condition rarities.

1920

Obverse 2 is the normal type for 1920, and so far, Obverse 3 is believed to be unique for 1920 — which makes it a very exciting find if another turns up!

Both Obverses are used in 1921, so there is an easy way to familiarise yourself with the two types: go through your 1921 pennies and identify the Obverse 2s and Obverse 3s. You can look at all the photographs you like, but there really is no substitute for examining coins in hand when learning varieties of this subtlety.

Obverses

  • ■ Obverse 2 – Lower colon dot is almost in contact with the letters on either side
  • ■ Obverse 3 – A and B are further apart, leaving a clear gap between the lower colon dot and the letters

1922

A very rare variety of the 1922 penny does exist, sometimes catalogued (inaccurately) as “1922 with 1927 reverse.”

Only a handful of these are known, but as with all relatively recent discoveries, there will almost certainly be more out there — so don’t stop checking for this one. I use the trident as a quick check on any 1922 pennies that I come across, but see how many other variations between the two reverses you can spot — it’s like a game of “spot the difference.”

There are some pennis which have a dot on the central prong of the trident.

1926

The 1926 Modified Head Penny makes up approximately 2.4% of the 1926 issue, according to Court’s survey — equivalent to about 1 in every 40 pennies — so it’s worth checking for.

This is one of those varieties that you learn to spot quickly, but here we’ll highlight the key diagnostics. The design change was part of a series of attempts to reduce the “ghosting” effect seen on many George V coins, where a faint inverse impression of the bust is visible on the reverse.
